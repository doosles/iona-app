Two-tap "I need help" — three shapes

Ruling 4 (21 Jul): the in-app help button becomes two-tap to activate; the orb stays one-tap; the Flic is unchanged. No clock on the confirm — the member may take as long as they like. Amendment 2's one-tap CANCEL during the countdown is untouched. Each option below shows tap 1 → the state it leaves behind. Nothing here is built.

A · The button arms itself

The button stays the button. Tap 1 fills it and changes the word; tap 2 activates. One target, same place, no new furniture — the member's finger never has to move. "Not now" sits underneath, quiet and unmissable.

C · Full-card takeover

Tap 1 replaces the message card entirely, so there is nothing else on screen to mis-hit. The most unmissable, and the most disruptive — it briefly hides the message the member was answering. Offered for completeness; heaviest of the three.

CC leans to A. It is the only one that is literally "tap it twice" — which is the sentence the ruling's own user-guide test uses ("Tap 'I need help' twice to set off an alarm"). The target never moves, so a member who has already decided is not asked to find a new button; the fill and the word change carry the confirmation instead. B is the safer-feeling option if you want the member to read a question before anything happens, and it borrows a pattern they have already met — but it turns one gesture into two different targets. C is the most emphatic and the most disruptive; it hides the message being answered.

Open in all three (owner-reserved, copy session): the armed wording — "Tap again to get help" / "Yes, get help" — and the escape wording — "Not now" / "Cancel".
Behaviour common to whichever is chosen: no clock on the confirm; the armed state clears if the member leaves the screen or taps the escape; tap 2 enters the existing cancel window exactly as today (siren → tone → Oran → countdown → one-tap cancel, Amendment 2 untouched); the orb and the Flic keep firing on one press.
